Problem

Vermont Reality

Homelessness in Vermont is a housing issue, a public health issue, and a discharge planning issue at the same time.

Housing shortage is the primary driver

Lack of affordable housing and poverty are key drivers of homelessness in Vermont.

Vermont has one of the highest rates of homelessness in the country, with 53 people experiencing homelessness per 10,000 residents compared to 22.7 nationally, ranking fourth overall in 2024. The state has seen continued increases among vulnerable populations, including a 36 percent rise in homelessness among families with children from 2022 to 2023. Structural factors, particularly a shortage of affordable housing and persistent poverty, are consistently identified as the primary drivers of homelessness statewide. Housing instability extends beyond those currently unhoused, with 10 percent of Vermonters aged 45 to 59 and 3 percent of those aged 60 and older experiencing housing insecurity. Disparities persist, with higher rates of housing insecurity among Black, Indigenous, and people of color compared to White residents.

Health Impact

Why a lack of housing quickly becomes a health care problem

Click each issue area to show the kind of placeholder explanation you can later replace with data, clinical framing, and citations.

Patients experiencing homelessness are often discharged to shelters or the street, environments that lack the basic conditions needed for recovery such as rest, hygiene, and safe wound care. Hospitals are not designed to address these needs, and discharge options are limited for this population. As a result, recovery is frequently disrupted immediately after discharge. This contributes to higher hospital readmission rates, with studies showing 27.3% readmission among unhoused patients compared to 17.5% for housed patients.

Managing medications and treatment plans is significantly more difficult without stable housing. Many patients lack access to refrigeration, secure storage, and consistent daily routines needed to take medications correctly. Follow-up care is also harder to access, with housing-insecure adults over four times more likely to delay healthcare due to cost. These barriers reduce treatment effectiveness and increase the risk of complications after discharge.

People experiencing homelessness have substantially higher rates of emergency department visits and hospital readmissions than housed populations. Studies show 30-day readmission rates exceeding 25%, and emergency department revisit rates of 37.1% compared to 23.6% for housed patients. This pattern reflects gaps in access to primary care and recovery support after discharge. It also drives higher healthcare costs and system strain.

Homelessness is associated with higher rates of chronic illness, mental health conditions, and substance use disorders. Among low-income Vermonters, rates of chronic conditions are significantly elevated, including 56% with a disability and 40% with arthritis. These conditions require consistent management, which is difficult without stable housing. As a result, health conditions worsen over time and lead to repeated use of emergency and inpatient care.

Patients experiencing homelessness often move through a repeating cycle of hospital care, discharge, and rapid return because the system is not designed to support recovery without stable housing. Hospital discharge processes are frequently ill-suited for this population, with limited options for patients who cannot safely recover in traditional home settings. After discharge, many patients are unable to access follow-up care, with studies showing significantly lower rates of post-discharge physician visits among people experiencing homelessness. Without continuity of care or a stable environment to complete treatment, existing conditions can worsen and new complications can emerge. Patients are then forced to rely on emergency care when symptoms become acute again. This creates a predictable cycle in which discharge does not lead to recovery, but instead leads back to the hospital.

Gap

Vermont Case Study: Motel Program

The motel program helps show why shelter matters and why shelter alone does not finish the job.

What Vermont’s motel program did well

Vermont’s motel program rapidly expanded access to indoor shelter during the COVID-19 pandemic, housing around 2,000 people at a time and using more than 1,000 rooms statewide. It prioritized medically vulnerable individuals and families, providing private rooms that improved safety, stability, and protection from exposure. In many ways, motel placements created conditions similar to recovery environments, including rest, privacy, and consistent indoor space. As an emergency response, it demonstrated how providing space can immediately reduce harm and meet urgent shelter needs.

Where the model fell short

The motel program was designed as short-term emergency housing, with time limits and eligibility rules that created instability and frequent exits from shelter. While motel rooms provided space, the program did not include clinical care, care coordination, or structured recovery support. It also relied heavily on pandemic-era funding and emergency policies, making it difficult to sustain in a post-COVID system. These limitations revealed the gap between providing shelter and supporting recovery.

Vermont’s motel program makes clear that expanding shelter capacity alone does not address the full scope of homelessness as a health issue. While the program successfully demonstrated the importance of providing private, indoor space, it also showed that space alone is not enough to support recovery after illness or hospitalization. Without systems for medical care, follow-up, and care coordination, the benefits of shelter are often temporary. The program also highlighted how emergency responses built around short-term funding and eligibility rules can create instability rather than continuity. As a result, it exposed a structural gap between emergency housing and recovery-focused care. This distinction is critical for policy, as it shows that addressing homelessness requires not only more shelter, but also systems designed to support health and recovery over time.

Solution

Medical Respite Care

Short-term medical recovery housing for people experiencing homelessness after hospital discharge.

Medical respite is not a hospital replacement and not simply a shelter bed. It is a bridge.

Medical respite care provides short-term residential recovery for people experiencing homelessness who are too sick to recover on the street but not sick enough to remain in the hospital. It serves as a bridge between hospital discharge and full recovery, offering a safe place where patients can rest, stabilize, and complete treatment. Unlike emergency shelter, medical respite includes clinical care, care coordination, and support for managing medications and follow-up appointments. Patients typically stay for several weeks, allowing time to recover from acute illness or injury while reconnecting to ongoing care. By filling the gap between acute care and housing instability, medical respite helps improve recovery and prevent avoidable returns to the hospital.

Evidence

What changes when recovery support exists

The evidence case can later combine outcomes, cost findings, and discharge planning implications in one clear narrative.

  • Repeat ER use
  • Longer recovery
  • Higher hospital costs
  • Fewer hospital admissions
  • Shorter inpatient stays
  • Better connection to outpatient care
  • Cost savings

Evidence across multiple studies shows that medical respite care reduces hospital use while improving system efficiency after discharge. In a cohort study, only 28% of patients receiving respite care were rehospitalized within a year, compared to 61% of those receiving usual care, indicating a substantial reduction in repeat hospitalizations . A separate analysis found that discharge to respite care was associated with a 46% lower odds of 90-day hospital readmission compared to discharge to the street or shelter . Systematic review evidence further shows consistent reductions in hospital admissions, inpatient days, and readmissions across programs, alongside improved housing outcomes after discharge . At the same time, hospitalizations for patients experiencing homelessness cost on average $2,559 more per stay than for housed patients, largely due to extended lengths of stay when safe discharge options are unavailable . Together, these findings show that medical respite does not just reduce utilization, but improves how and where care is delivered after hospitalization.

Policy

Policy Proposal

A Vermont strategy can start with a pilot, build partnerships, and create a stronger discharge pathway over time.

1

Fund regional pilot programs

Start with a small number of regional pilots in areas where hospital demand and homelessness overlap to test the model and generate Vermont-specific data.

2

Partner hospitals with community health centers

Establish formal referral and care pathways between hospitals and community providers to ensure patients receive coordinated follow-up after discharge.

3

Use Medicaid Section 1115 waiver authority where possible

Leverage Medicaid flexibility to fund care coordination, case management, and recovery services that improve health outcomes and reduce costs.

4

Add medical respite referral into hospital discharge planning

Integrate respite care as a standard discharge option for eligible patients to ensure safe recovery and reduce avoidable readmissions.

A Vermont medical respite strategy can begin with a targeted regional pilot that focuses on areas with high hospital utilization and limited discharge options. A pilot approach allows the state to test program design, identify the patients who benefit most, and measure impacts on hospital use and costs before scaling. Successful implementation will require coordination between hospitals, community health providers, housing organizations, and state agencies to ensure continuity of care after discharge. Existing Medicaid pathways, including Section 1115 waivers, can be used to support care coordination and recovery services tied to health outcomes. Embedding medical respite into discharge planning ensures that patients have a safe place to recover, reducing avoidable hospital returns. Over time, data from the pilot can guide expansion and help build a more effective, statewide recovery system.

Policy Goal

What the proposal is asking for

  • Pilot medical respite regionally.
  • Connect hospitals with community care partners.
  • Measure health outcomes and cost effects.
  • Use results to guide future scaling.

Call to Action

Recovery shouldn’t depend on having a home.

Vermont policymakers can close the gap between hospital discharge and homelessness by treating recovery support as part of the health care system, not as an afterthought. Medical respite will not solve every housing challenge, but it can create a more humane, practical, and evidence-ready response for patients who are otherwise left without a safe place to heal.
